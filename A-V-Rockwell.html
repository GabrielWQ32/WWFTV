A. V. Rockwell

Life & Career

Athena Vanessa Rockwell (known professionally as A. V. Rockwell) is an American filmmaker and screenwriter whose work has been celebrated for its richly textured storytelling and evocative exploration of community, memory, and identity. Born and raised in Brooklyn, New York, Rockwell’s early life was suffused with the rhythms of city streets and the vibrant cultural tapestry of her neighborhood. The daughter of Afro‑Dominican and African‑American parents, she straddled multiple heritages, developing an acute sensitivity to questions of displacement, belonging, and intergenerational legacy.

Rockwell pursued her undergraduate studies at New York University’s Tisch School of the Arts, where she earned a BFA in Film & TV. There she was mentored by influential independent filmmakers who encouraged her to imbue her work with personal truth and formal experimentation. After graduation, she spent several years directing short films and documentaries that screened at festivals such as Sundance, Tribeca, and SXSW. These early works—often self‑financed and produced outside the studio system—demonstrated her gift for blending lyrical visuals with intimate, character‑driven narratives. She became known for her warm yet unflinching lens on everyday lives, capturing small moments that resonate with universal poignancy.

In 2019, Rockwell wrote and directed her debut feature, Darius’ Entrance, a semi‑autobiographical drama following a young man navigating unexpected grief and the bittersweet joys of first love in a gentrifying neighborhood. The film premiered to critical acclaim at the Sundance Film Festival, where reviewers praised its “quiet power” and “humanistic vision.” Rockwell’s screenplay, marked by naturalistic dialogue and a layered sense of place, won the Best Screenplay award in Sundance’s U.S. Dramatic Competition.

Following that success, Rockwell was tapped by streaming platforms to develop original series. She served as showrunner and lead writer on the critically lauded anthology drama Brooklyn Souls (2021–2022), which interwove stories of three generations of a Caribbean‑American family confronting legacy, ambition, and forgiveness. Under her guidance, the writers’ room was populated predominantly by women and writers of color, reflecting Rockwell’s commitment to inclusive storytelling both on‑screen and behind the scenes.

Beyond her creative projects, Rockwell is a dedicated educator and advocate. She teaches screenwriting workshops at her alma mater, NYU, and serves on the board of the Brooklyn Filmmakers Collective, a nonprofit supporting emerging filmmakers from marginalized communities. Rockwell’s essays on representation and authorship have appeared in Film Comment and IndieWire, and she regularly mentors young writers through the Sundance Institute’s Catalyst Program. Her career reflects a seamless integration of artistry, activism, and mentorship, cementing her reputation as one of her generation’s most vital voices.

Major Works & Legacy

Darius’ Entrance (2019) Rockwell’s debut feature introduces Darius Carter, a college‑bound teenager whose mother’s sudden death forces him to confront loss in the very Brooklyn block where they lived. Rockwell structures the film around small rituals—recording morning prayers on a handheld camera, sharing late‑night conversations on stoops—that underscore the beauty and fragility of ordinary moments. The screenplay deftly balances humor and heartbreak, earning a Special Jury Prize for Ensemble Performance at Sundance and a nomination for the Independent Spirit Award for Best First Screenplay.

Brooklyn Souls (2021–2022) This four‑episode anthology series examines the lives of three generations of the Rosario family: the matriarch who immigrated from the Dominican Republic in the 1970s, her daughter balancing career and motherhood in the 1990s, and her granddaughter grappling with digital alienation in the 2010s. Each chapter is written by Rockwell, who centers Afro‑Latinx voices and interlaces archival footage, home movies, and original score to create a tapestry of memory and resilience. The series received multiple Emmy nominations—including Outstanding Limited Series and Outstanding Writing for a Limited Series—and was lauded for its nuanced portrayal of diaspora identity.

Short Films & Documentaries Before her feature debut, Rockwell directed acclaimed shorts such as Morning Light (2014), a 15‑minute film capturing sunrise rituals in a senior living community, and Streets Unheard (2016), a vérité‑style documentary about subway musicians in Queens. Both shorts won awards at the Tribeca Film Festival and were acquired by PBS for national broadcast.

Collaborative Projects Rockwell contributed story and script work to Ava DuVernay’s anthology series When We Rise (2017), penning the episode dramatizing early AIDS activism in San Francisco. She also co‑wrote the screenplay for Concrete Lullabies (2020), a Netflix‑commissioned film about a father‑daughter road trip across the American South, which premiered at Cannes’ Directors’ Fortnight.

Rockwell’s influence lies not only in her own filmography but in her insistence on authentic collaboration and community engagement. By opening her creative process to emerging voices, she has helped reshape indie filmmaking in Brooklyn into a more diverse and supportive ecosystem. Her work is studied in film schools for its blend of vérité realism and poetic storytelling, and her production model—small budgets, tight-knit crews, nonprofit partnerships—has become a blueprint for sustainable independent cinema.
