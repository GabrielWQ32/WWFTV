Carrie Fisher

Life & Career

Carrie Fisher (1956–2016) was born in Beverly Hills into a Hollywood family—her parents were Debbie Reynolds and Eddie Fisher—but she carved out her own remarkable path as an actress and writer. At age 19, Fisher burst onto the global stage as Princess Leia Organa in George Lucas’s original Star Wars (1977), instantly becoming an icon of strength, wit, and resilience. Behind the scenes, she battled personal struggles and turned to writing as a form of self‑expression and healing.

Fisher’s gift for razor‑sharp dialogue and incisive observation led her to pen the semi‑autobiographical novel Postcards from the Edge (1987), based on her own experiences with addiction and Hollywood pressures. She adapted it for the screen in 1990, earning widespread acclaim for her candid, darkly comedic voice. Over the years she continued to write screenplays, essays, and memoirs, while also reprising Leia in multiple Star Wars sequels, demonstrating a rare versatility and enduring connection with audiences.

Major Works & Legacy

Fisher’s debut novel Postcards from the Edge remains a landmark in celebrity memoir and fiction, blending humor and heartbreak in a way only she could. Her screenplay adaptation was praised for its honesty and sharp wit. She co‑wrote the screenplay for the fantasy comedy Hook (1991) and contributed to the script of Scream 3 (2000), showcasing her range across genres.

Beyond her published work, Fisher became a beloved cultural commentator, known for her candor on mental health and addiction. Her posthumous memoir The Princess Diarist (2016) offered further insight into her life and work, solidifying her reputation as a fearless truth‑teller. Carrie Fisher’s legacy endures in her portrayal of Leia—an enduring symbol of empowerment—and in her writing, which continues to inspire new generations to speak their truth with wit and courage.
