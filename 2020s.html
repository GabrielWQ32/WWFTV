2020s: Women’s Screenwriting in Film & TV

← Back to Home

The early 2020s continue to witness a seismic shift in how stories are told and consumed, with women screenwriters at the forefront of streaming, global cinema, and genre reinvention. Amid a pandemic‑driven surge in streaming, unprecedented industry challenges, and a renewed focus on diversity, female writers are crafting work that bridges cultural divides and redefines narrative norms. This article examines the transformative contributions of women writers in film and television from 2020 to the present.

Film: Global Voices and Genre Evolution

Emerald Fennell’s Promising Young Woman (2020), which won her the Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay, demonstrated the power of female-authored social commentary in a thriller format. Fennell’s script confronted sexual violence and systemic misogyny with dark humor and bold narrative twists, marking a watershed moment for women writers in Hollywood.

In international cinema, Lina Wertmüller’s protégé Francesca Archibugi’s Momenti di trascurabile felicità (2020) showcased Italian female storytelling in comedy-drama, while South Korean writer-director Lee Chang-dong’s frequent collaborators – including female writers like Kim Min-hee – scripted films such as Decision to Leave (2022), blending romance, crime, and cultural nuance.

Women continue to break genre boundaries in superhero narratives: Women like Victoria Mahoney co-directed second-unit sequences for major blockbusters (Star Wars: The Rise of Skywalker 2019) and began writing for upcoming Marvel projects, ensuring diverse perspectives in big-budget franchises.

On the independent front, Regina Pessoa’s collaboration on animated features such as Unicorn Wars (2022) highlighted women’s roles in pushing animated storytelling into darker, more auteur-driven territory.

TV: Streaming, Diverse Platforms, and New Formats

Michaela Coel’s I May Destroy You (2020) cemented her status as a leading writer-producer, earning Emmy and BAFTA awards. Her unflinching narrative structure, co-written with a team of women such as Elle-Máijá Tailfeathers, addressed consent and trauma with unprecedented candor on HBO.

Streaming platforms have commissioned an array of female-authored series: Phoebe Waller-Bridge returned with Killing Eve seasons with co-showrunner Emerald Fennell, blending espionage and female rivalry. Shonda Rhimes’s company continued to lead Netflix’s slate with series like Bridgerton (2020–), scripted by women including Jess Brownell, redefining period romance with a modern lens.

In genre television, Nia DaCosta co-wrote early episodes of Marvel’s Moon Knight (2022), while women writers on The Witcher Netflix series—such as Declan de Barra and Lauren S. Hissrich—incorporated deeper female character arcs, ensuring women’s voices in fantasy world-building.

Documentary series have also flourished: Ava DuVernay’s When They See Us (2019) led to female-led docu-reentries like Allen v. Farrow (2021), co-written by filmmakers such as Larissa Rhodes, exploring gender, power, and narrative control in true-crime formats.

Industry and Cultural Shifts

The Writers Guild strikes and global calls for equity in 2023 underscored ongoing battles for fair pay and representation. Initiatives like the WGA’s inclusion efforts and the ICC’s gender parity pledges in Europe have accelerated women’s access to writers’ rooms across continents.

Pandemic-era writing rooms adapted to remote collaboration, with female showrunners leveraging virtual platforms to build international writer teams, democratizing entry points and fostering cross-cultural storytelling partnerships.

Looking Ahead

As we navigate the mid-2020s, women screenwriters continue redefining cinematic and televisual norms, from AI-assisted writing explorations by writers like Lucy Prebble to transmedia narratives across gaming and interactive streaming. Their voices remain central to how stories evolve in an ever-changing industry—ensuring that WWFTV’s celebration of women’s screenwriting continues to resonate globally.
