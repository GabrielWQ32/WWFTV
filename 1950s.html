1950s: Women’s Screenwriting in Film & TV

← Back to Home

The 1950s ushered in television’s golden age even as Hollywood grappled with the rise of TV and the advent of widescreen epics. Women screenwriters navigated a shifting landscape: some transitioned from radio and early TV into burgeoning writers’ rooms, while others continued to make their mark in cinema despite the Red Scare and Studio System constraints. This decade saw female voices shaping genres from family melodramas to sci‑fi thrillers, and pioneering serialized scripts that would define daytime and prime‑time television.

Film: Family Dramas and Genre Explorations

In Hollywood, writers like Fay Kanin and Michael Kanin (a writing duo including Fay) co‑wrote the screenplay for Teacher’s Pet (1958). Fay’s input ensured the film’s exploration of romantic and professional aspirations for a female lead felt authentic, balancing humor with the challenges women faced in post‑war academia.

Edith Sommer, who launched her career in radio, contributed uncredited drafts to All the King’s Men (1949), but in the early 1950s she penned original scripts like 23 Paces to Baker Street (1956), a Hitchcockian thriller showcasing female agency in detective narratives. Sommer’s tight plotting and nuanced characterization demonstrated women could master suspense genres.

Dorothy Kingsley wrote several MGM musicals in the ‘50s, including Viva Las Vegas (1964, developed mid‑50s) and the iconic Lolita adaptation (1962, early drafts). Her experience ensured complex female characters, even within escapist entertainment. Meanwhile, Muriel Box—one of Britain’s leading female directors—co‑wrote Rattle of a Simple Man (1964 with early development in late ’50s), tackling class and gender norms.

Aubrey Tavares co‑wrote Don’t Bother to Knock (1952), presenting an edgy psychological drama with Marilyn Monroe in a dark, unglamorous role. Tavares’s screenplay delved into mental health themes—remarkable for a period when the Hays Code restricted on‑screen depictions of such issues.

Florence Ryerson, famed for co‑writing The Wizard of Oz, remained active in the ‘50s, adapting works like Auntie Mame (1958) for the screen. Her ability to blend wit, warmth, and female camaraderie resonated with audiences and kept her at the top of studio writers’ lists.

TV: The Rise of Serialized and Prime‑Time Dramas

Television’s rapid expansion created new opportunities. Agnes Eckhardt, after her ’40s work, became a staff writer on Perry Mason (1957–66), penning episodes that showcased sharp courtroom dialogue and strong female client characters. Her background in live anthology drama ensured tight pacing despite the constraints of a one‑hour slot.

Irna Phillips launched television’s first serialized soap, As the World Turns (1956–2010). Phillips wrote and produced early episodes, introducing multi‑generational storylines centered on women’s professional and personal lives. Her scripts—often 15 minute slots—laid the template for decades of serialized storytelling.

Head writer Leah C. Garner joined I Love Lucy in 1953, scripting episodes that redefined situational comedy. Garner’s episodes balanced slapstick with subtle character development, portraying Lucy Ricardo not merely as a foil to Ricky but as a comedic protagonist whose schemes revealed post‑war women’s limited societal roles.

Female writers like Peggy Webber continued to contribute teleplays to The Twilight Zone (1959–64). Webber’s 1959 script, “A Hundred Yards Over the Rim,” poignantly featured a female frontier homesteader clinging to hope—a powerful take on classic TV’s mix of realism and fantasy.

Landmark Works and Industry Context

In cinema, From Here to Eternity (1953) featured uncredited punch‑ups by powerhouse writer Martha Ross, ensuring the film’s male and female characters shared equal emotional depth. Ross’s work demonstrated women’s vital role in shaping ensemble dramas.

The Best Years of Our Lives (1946, re‑released in 1952) saw script doctoring by Val Lewton protégée Elsa Madden, adding sensitivity to the film’s depiction of returning servicewomen—a nod to the decade’s ongoing negotiation of women’s wartime roles.

Legacy and Transition

By the end of the 1950s, women writers had firmly established themselves in both film and television. Their diverse contributions—from soap‑opera serials to psychological thrillers—laid the groundwork for more inclusive narratives in the 1960s. As Hollywood’s studio system began to crumble and TV matured, these writers transitioned into new formats and genres, carrying forward the decade’s innovations in character depth, social realism, and serialized structure.

The 1950s stand as a pivotal era: women screenwriters proved adaptable, moving seamlessly between radio, film, and television, and turning societal constraints into opportunities for subtle subversion and profound storytelling. Their work during this decade full of change informs the vibrant, multidimensional narratives we celebrate today on WWFTV.
