Our Mission

Women Writers Of Film & Television exists to shine a powerful spotlight on the voices, visions, and victories of women screenwriters—past, present, and future. Our mission unfolds in four intertwined pillars: 1. Preserve & Celebrate History We collect and curate the stories of trailblazing women whose scripts shaped cinema and television—from the pioneering work of Alice Guy‑Blaché in the 1910s to the boundary‑breaking screenplays of today. Through archival partnerships, oral‑history projects, and digital exhibits, we safeguard their contributions so they can’t be forgotten, ensuring every young writer can stand on the shoulders of giants. 2. Amplify Contemporary Voices By hosting live readings, panel discussions, and online showcases, we give emerging and established women writers a platform to share their work with global audiences. We partner with festivals, studios, and virtual communities to broadcast new voices—fostering an ecosystem where diverse perspectives are not just heard, but celebrated. 3. Educate & Empower We believe knowledge is power. Our workshops, masterclasses, and mentorship programs connect aspiring screenwriters with industry veterans—providing concrete skills in story structure, character development, and negotiation, as well as guidance on navigating a traditionally male‑dominated field. Scholarships, grant opportunities, and script labs break down financial barriers and make the craft accessible to everyone. 4. Advocate for Equity Real change requires action. We campaign for fair hiring practices, transparent pay scales, and inclusive writers’ rooms. By publishing annual research reports, engaging with guilds and studios, and mobilizing our membership, we press for policies that ensure women writers receive the recognition, resources, and respect they deserve. Together, through preservation, celebration, education, and advocacy, Women Writers Of Film & Television is building a world where every woman with a story to tell can bring her vision to the screen—and where that vision has the chance to change hearts, minds, and the course of cinema history.

Spotlight: Nora Ephron

Nora Ephron (1941–2012) was an award‑nominated American screenwriter, director, and producer whose razor‑sharp wit and keen observations on modern relationships forever changed romantic comedy. She penned classics like When Harry Met Sally… (1989) and Sleepless in Seattle (1993), crafting dialogue that feels at once effortlessly natural and delightfully clever. A three‑time Academy Award nominee (including Best Original Screenplay for Silkwood), Ephron’s work often wove in themes of female empowerment, self‑discovery, and the humor found in everyday life. Beyond the page and screen, she was a bestselling author and journalist, celebrated for her essay collections and cultural commentary.

Milestones in Women’s Screenwriting

1896

Georges Méliès employs his first female collaborator, Jeanne d’Alcy, in film writing.

1910

Alice Guy‑Blaché writes and directs “The Cabbage Fairy,” one of the first narrative films by a woman.

1917

Anita Loos publishes “Gentlemen Prefer Blondes,” later adapted by Hollywood.

1927

Frances Marion becomes the first woman to win an Academy Award for Writing: “The Big House.”

1934

Lillian Hellman’s screenplay for “The Little Foxes” premieres on Broadway before film adaptation.

1939

Ida Lupino co‑writes and directs “While You Were Out,” paving the way for female directors/writers.

1951

Dorothy Parker co‑writes the script for “A Star Is Born,” showcasing sharp comedic dialogue.

1974

Marion Zimmer Bradley adapts “The Mists of Avalon,” blending feminist themes in fantasy.

1979

Nancy Dowd wins the Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay for “Coming Home.”

1983

Nora Ephron pens “Silkwood,” earning an Oscar nomination for Best Adapted Screenplay.

1990

Lina Wertmüller becomes the first woman nominated for an Academy Award for Best Director with “Seven Beauties,” also co‑writing the script.

1994

Emma Thompson wins both an Oscar for Best Actress and Best Original Screenplay for “Sense and Sensibility.”

2003

Deborah Moggach’s screenplay adaptation for “The Best Exotic Marigold Hotel” highlights cross‑cultural narratives.

2009

Greta Gerwig co‑writes “Frances Ha,” later becoming a defining voice in indie cinema.

2017

Jordan Peele’s “Get Out” is co‑written with female collaborators, blending horror & social satire.

2019

Emerald Fennell wins the Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay for “Promising Young Woman.”

2022

Jane Campion wins Cannes’ Palme d’Or for “The Power of the Dog” (adapted by her).
