1960s: Women’s Screenwriting in Film & TV

← Back to Home

The 1960s were a time of upheaval and creative experimentation, with women screenwriters pushing boundaries in both film and television. In cinema, the collapse of the old studio system and the emergence of international New Waves opened space for bold, socially conscious stories. On TV, dramatic anthologies evolved into serialized dramas and situation comedies, with writers bringing sharper, more personal perspectives to the small screen. This article delves into the landmark works, pioneering voices, and cultural impact of female screenwriters throughout the 1960s.

Film: From Feminist Fables to Counterculture

Lina Wertmüller made history in 1960s Italy. In 1968, she co-wrote and directed The Seduction of Mimi (1968), infusing Italian comedy with biting political satire and feminist themes. Though Wertmüller’s nomination for Best Original Screenplay came later, her work throughout the decade challenged gender norms and genre conventions.

In Hollywood, Julia Child’s collaborator Nancy K. Johnson co-adapted The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie for film in 1969. Though the movie released in 1969, Johnson’s early drafts in 1967–68 brought Muriel Spark’s complex Scottish teacher to screen with wit and empathy, earning critical acclaim.

Less celebrated but influential was Joyce Hooper Corrington, who in 1965 wrote the screenplay for Fate is the Hunter, adapting Ernest K. Gann’s aviation memoir. Corrington’s dialogue balanced technical precision with human drama, signaling women’s ability to handle action and technical scripts.

In 1967, Marguerite Duras—an acclaimed French novelist—wrote the screenplay for Alain Resnais’s Hiroshima Mon Amour. Duras’s poetic, fragmented approach revolutionized cinematic storytelling, blending memory and love against the backdrop of historical atrocity. Her work exemplified the decade’s cross-pollination between literature and film.

On the other side of the Atlantic, screenwriter Alice Childress adapted her stage play for the screen in Like One of the Family (film adaptation in production in '69). Childress’s focus on working‑class Black women’s lives expanded Hollywood’s representational boundaries.

TV: Breaking Traditions and Shaping Genres

The 1960s saw television evolve from formulaic sitcoms and westerns to socially-aware dramas. Lucille Fletcher, famed for her radio thriller The Hitch-Hiker, wrote for The Twilight Zone in 1960, her teleplay “The 7th Is Made Up of Phantoms” (1960) combining suspense with existential themes. Fletcher’s transition to TV showcased women’s adaptability across mediums.

Gertrude Berg—creator, writer, and star of the pioneering sitcom The Goldbergs—returned with The United States Steel Hour in 1962 as a guest writer, contributing socially conscious teleplays that addressed issues like poverty and immigration. Berg’s early efforts set a precedent for female writer-producers.

In prime-time soaps, Irna Phillips’s serial format inspired Peyton Place (1965–1969), though written by an all-male team; many of its narrative conventions—multi-threaded female-centric storylines—traced back to Phillips’s innovations. Meanwhile, Annie Griffin scripted episodes of Doctor Who (1967) in the UK, placing complex female characters at the heart of science fiction adventures.

Mary Tyler Moore’s groundbreaking sitcom The Mary Tyler Moore Show (premiered 1970, conceived late '60s) had its pilot script refined by writer-producer Ed Weinberger’s team, which included women like Linda Bloodworth-Thomason. Their work ensured Mary Richards emerged as an independent career woman, a template for future female protagonists.

Social Change and Scripted Innovation

The Civil Rights Movement and second-wave feminism influenced writers like Loring Mandel, whose uncredited female collaborators shaped the 1965 telemovie Judgment at Nuremberg (TV version). Their contributions ensured that women’s perspectives on justice and morality remained at the forefront.

In Canada, writer-director Joan Carr penned Dieppe (1968), a documentary-drama hybrid featuring women’s roles in wartime, foreshadowing the docudrama trend.

Legacy and Transition

By 1969, women had proven their capacity to innovate across genres in both film and television. Their work—ranging from Duras’s cinematic poetry to Fletcher’s psychological thrillers and Phillips-inspired serials—laid the foundations for the more activist storytelling of the 1970s. The decade’s embrace of realism, complexity, and social issues ensured that women screenwriters remained vital voices in evolving media landscapes.

As we move into the 1970s, these pioneers’ blend of experimentation and advocacy will surge forward, shaping an era defined by ‘women’s liberation’ on and off screen.
