Abby Ajayi

Life & Career

Abby Ajayi was born in London in 1986 to Nigerian immigrant parents who valued education, community, and storytelling. From her earliest days she was surrounded by the rhythms and narratives of two cultures: the oral traditions and folktales her grandparents shared, and the city’s multicultural tapestry. She studied English literature at King’s College London, where she wrote articles and short stories for the student magazine, experimenting with voice and form to capture the diasporic experience. After graduating in 2007, Ajayi entered the competitive world of publishing, working first in the editorial department of a major trade press, then as a researcher for documentary television, where she honed her skills in narrative structure, factual accuracy, and collaborative writing.

In 2012, Ajayi made the leap to drama, joining the writers’ room on a BBC anthology series, contributing her first television scripts. Her breakthrough came in 2015 when she co‑created and co‑wrote the critically acclaimed miniseries Crossroads & Coconuts, which explored three generations of a Caribbean family in London, interweaving supernatural folklore with contemporary issues of identity, belonging, and mental health. The series was praised for its evocative dialogue—at once lyrical and grounded—and its confident blending of genres, from urban fantasy to family drama.

Following that success, Ajayi was commissioned to adapt Zadie Smith’s novel NW for the screen. Her adaptation, broadcast in 2018, captured the book’s fractured narrative through a mosaic of interlocking monologues, visual flashbacks, and split‑screen sequences, preserving the novel’s jazzlike structure. Critics and audiences lauded her ability to honor the source while translating it into a dynamic, cinematic language.

In 2019, Ajayi was named a Screen International Star of Tomorrow and invited to the Sundance Screenwriters Lab, where she developed her first original feature screenplay, Blue Hibiscus, a coming‑of‑age story set in postcolonial Nigeria and London. The film explores the tensions between tradition and modernity, family loyalty and personal freedom, as seen through the eyes of a restless teenager returning to Lagos to prepare for university. Ajayi’s script won the Waldo Salt Screenwriting Award at Sundance 2021, with judges praising its “rich cultural voice” and “fearless emotional honesty.”

Alongside her dramatic work, Ajayi has written essays and short fiction for The Guardian, Granta, and The New Yorker, often reflecting on migration, memory, and the intersections of race and gender. She lectures on screenwriting at the National Film and Television School in the U.K., mentoring emerging writers and advocating for greater diversity in writers’ rooms. She is a trustee of the British Blacklist Foundation, which funds and supports Black British screenwriters.

Major Works & Legacy

Crossroads & Coconuts (2015) As co‑creator and lead writer, Ajayi shaped this three‑episode BBC miniseries that follows the wealthy matriarch Viola Cross, her estranged daughter Tanisha, and grandson Kaleb as they contend with ancestral spirits, long‑buried secrets, and the burdens of inheritance. Ajayi’s scripts balance eerie supernatural elements—visions of past wrongs—with intimate scenes of family conflict, set against the vibrant backdrop of south London. The series won the BAFTA for Best Mini‑Series and was celebrated for broadening the scope of British supernatural drama to include African folklore.

NW (2018) Adapting Zadie Smith’s complex, non‑linear novel, Ajayi faced the challenge of shifting between multiple protagonists—Leah, Natalie, Kelvin, and Felix—and three distinct London precincts. Her screenplay employs inventive devices: interior monologues voiced over montage, split‑screen conversations, and seamless temporal jumps. The result preserved the novel’s rhythmic energy and thematic depth. Ajayi received the Writer’s Guild Award for Best TV Adaptation for her work.

Blue Hibiscus (2021) Developed at Sundance and financed in 2022, this feature screenplay centers on Amara, a Nigerian‑British student who returns home to Lagos following her grandmother’s death. As Amara navigates family expectations, cultural dislocation, and youthful rebellion, Ajayi weaves in Lagos’s pulsating street life, the legacy of colonial architecture, and the simmering tensions between Western modernity and Nigerian tradition. The script won the Humanitas Prize for its compassionate portrayal of cross‑cultural identity.

Short Films & Collaborations • Market Day (2013), a 12‑minute short about two sisters selling fish in Brick Lane market, won Best Short at Raindance Film Festival. • Between Worlds (2014), commissioned by the BBC’s Shorts strand, explores the afterlife beliefs of Ghanaian immigrants in London. • Consulting writer on Netflix’s Queen Charlotte (2023), shaping dialogue and character arcs in its second season.

Influence & Recognition Ajayi’s work is studied in film and literature courses for its genre‑defying fusion of folklore, literary adaptation, and social realism. She has been a keynote speaker at the BFI London Film Festival and named one of Variety’s “10 Screenwriters to Watch” in 2022. Her commitment to mentoring and diversity initiatives has helped usher in a new generation of U.K. screenwriters from underrepresented communities.

As a storyteller, Abby Ajayi has broadened the horizons of British television and film, demonstrating that narratives rooted in African and diasporic cultures can resonate globally. Her legacy is not only in the awards and critical acclaim but in the inclusive writers’ rooms she champions and the richly textured worlds she brings to life on screen.
