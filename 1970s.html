1970s: Women’s Screenwriting in Film & TV

← Back to Home

The 1970s heralded a dynamic shift as the second-wave feminist movement reshaped cultural landscapes. Women screenwriters seized newfound creative freedoms in both film and television, tackling gender roles, social justice, and personal narratives with unprecedented candor. Breaking away from studio constraints and network formulas, they authored stories that spoke directly to a generation demanding equality and representation. This article examines the most influential women writers of the 1970s and their landmark works in film and television.

Film: Feminist Perspectives and Genre Innovation

In 1974, Lillian Hellman’s produced screenplay for The Little Foxes adaptation bore female-driven moral complexity onto the big screen, but the watershed moment came with Lina Wertmüller’s Seven Beauties (1976). Wertmüller became the first woman nominated for the Academy Award for Best Director and Best Original Screenplay. The film’s blend of dark humor, political commentary, and unapologetic female agency exemplified the decade’s willingness to challenge cinematic norms.

Across the Atlantic, Elaine May’s co-written scripts continued to defy genre boundaries. Though her solo directorial debut Ishtar (1987) later flopped, her 1970s writing work on films like The Heartbreak Kid (1972, uncredited punch-up) showcased her trademark wit and subversion of romantic comedy conventions. May’s contributions ensured that female humorists remained central to Hollywood storytelling.

American independent cinema also benefited from Jane Wagner’s collaboration with Lily Tomlin. Wagner wrote the one-woman film Moment by Moment (1978), exploring female intimacy and aging. Though commercially modest, Wagner’s willingness to center mature women’s stories paved the way for more nuanced female protagonists.

Marlene Dietrich championed female writers into her late career, scripting her own onscreen persona with writer Ann-Marie Martin for the German fantasy Bluebeard (1972). Their collaboration highlighted women’s creative control at a time when aging actresses were often sidelined.

In 1979, Nancy Dowd won the Academy Award for Best Original Screenplay for Coming Home, a film examining veterans’ post‑war experiences and the Vietnam-era sexual revolution. Dowd’s script balanced political urgency with personal relationships, capturing women’s evolving roles in a society grappling with conflict and change.

TV: Diversification and Serialized Depth

Television in the 1970s matured beyond formulaic sitcoms and westerns. Showrunners like Susan Harris created The Golden Girls ensemble prototype in her 1977 sitcom Soap, though the popular series debuted in 1977. Harris’s irreverent satire tackled marriage, infidelity, and sexuality through four female leads. While controversial at launch, Soap demonstrated network willingness to explore taboo subjects with female-driven writers at the helm.

In drama, Mimi Leder cut her teeth as a story editor on The Wonder Years in the late ’80s, but her writing roots trace back to ABC’s 1974 anthology ABC Stage 67, where women like Joan Darling directed and wrote teleplays exploring feminism and family dynamics. Darling’s scripts for episodes such as “Doctor Peabody’s Improbable History” showcased early female voices shaping variety and drama formats.

The miniseries format burgeoned in the 1970s, and women played key roles. Judith Arias co‑wrote elements of Roots (1977) adaptation, focusing on female ancestral experiences, even if uncredited. Similarly, Blanche Hanalis adapted Laura Ingalls Wilder’s novels into the 1974 miniseries Little House on the Prairie, creating roles for strong frontier women that resonated across generations.

On soap operas, Agnes Nixon’s creation All My Children (1970–2011) became a landmark daytime drama. Nixon wrote early episodes that introduced issues like rape and women’s liberation to daytime TV, elevating soap operas into platforms for social commentary.

Landmark Episodes and Impact

In 1972, Gail Parent and Kathy Speer wrote the pilot for The Mary Tyler Moore Show, even as Linda Bloodworth‑Thomason contributed to later scripts. Their work redefined single, career-focused women on television, with Mary Richards’s newsroom exploits challenging gender norms.

Meanwhile, feminist filmmakers like Nora Ephron transitioned from print journalism into TV writing in the late ’70s, scripting early programs such as Saturday Night Live sketches and teleplays that honed her signature dialogue style—later perfected in her breakthrough film scripts.

Legacy and Forward Momentum

By decade’s end, women screenwriters had shifted entertainment’s center of gravity. Their films tackled war trauma, political satire, and female empowerment; their television scripts brought serialized social realism and comedic boldness to domestic screens. The 1970s set the stage for the blockbuster and prestige TV revolutions to come, ensuring that female writers would remain crucial voices in shaping popular culture.

As we enter the 1980s, these trailblazers’ influence will expand into new genres, from romantic comedy to independent cinema and cable television. The groundbreaking work of the 1970s ensures that women’s stories remain at the forefront of storytelling innovation.
